# What Day of the Week Was I Born?

"Monday's child is fair of face, Tuesday's child is full of grace…" — the old nursery rhyme has kept people curious about their birth weekday for generations. Whether you believe the rhyme or just want to know, finding the exact day of the week you were born is quick and satisfying. This guide shows you how it works, and a clever trick to work it out in your head.

## Every date has exactly one weekday

The calendar repeats on a seven-day cycle, so any given date — past or future — falls on exactly one day of the week. Computers determine this by counting the total number of days from a known reference date and taking the remainder after dividing by 7. Because the day count is exact and **leap-year aware**, the weekday is always correct, even for dates centuries ago.

That's precisely how the [Age Calculator](/tools/age-calculator) finds your birth weekday: it computes the exact calendar day and maps it to Sunday–Saturday.

## The Doomsday rule: work it out in your head

If you enjoy mental maths, the **Doomsday rule** (devised by mathematician John Conway) lets you find any date's weekday without a computer. The idea: in every year, a set of easy-to-remember dates all fall on the same weekday — the year's "Doomsday".

Memorable Doomsday dates include:

- **4/4, 6/6, 8/8, 10/10, 12/12** (even months matching their own number)
- **9/5, 5/9, 7/11, 11/7** ("9-to-5 at 7-11")
- The **last day of February** (28th or 29th)

Once you know a year's Doomsday weekday, you count forward or back from the nearest anchor date to your target date. With practice, people do this in a few seconds. It's a party trick — but the [Age Calculator](/tools/age-calculator) is faster and never slips.

## How leap years affect the weekday

Because a common year has 365 days (52 weeks + 1 day), the same date shifts forward by **one weekday** each year. But a leap year has 366 days (52 weeks + 2 days), so after a 29 February, dates jump forward by **two** weekdays. This is exactly why your birthday lands on a different day of the week each year, and why the pattern isn't a simple "+1 every year".

For example, a date might fall on Monday one year, Tuesday the next, then **skip to Thursday** across a leap year. Any accurate weekday calculation — including the one in the [Age Calculator](/tools/age-calculator) — accounts for this automatically.

A mental-maths method for finding any date's weekday by remembering a set of dates that share the same weekday within each year.
